Concentration measures how unevenly the communication load is distributed across actors. The Gini coefficient ranges from 0 (perfectly equal) to 1 (one actor receives all communication). High concentration creates structural vulnerability: when central actors are unavailable, communication pathways break down. Values above 0.6 indicate critical overload risk; values below 0.3 may signal a lack of coordination structure.

Key finding
Gini dropped after Skilling resignation (Aug 2001)
The Gini coefficient remained between 0.30–0.45 throughout the observation period, consistently in the yellow zone. After Skilling's resignation in August 2001, it temporarily dropped to 0.30, indicating a temporary redistribution of communication load. By January 2002 it recovered to 0.45–0.50, reflecting crisis coordination concentrated among a shrinking group of active actors.
Interpretation guide
Traffic light thresholds
0–0.3: Even distribution, no overload risk
0.3–0.6: Moderate concentration, some actors more central
0.6–1.0: High concentration, overload and bottleneck risk
Integration measures whether structurally distinct groups within the network remain connected through cross-boundary communication. Three metrics are used: the Silhouette score assesses cluster quality, the Inter-cluster ratio (ICR) measures the share of communication crossing cluster boundaries, and the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) tracks how stable cluster composition is over time. Declining ICR is the earliest warning signal of fragmentation.

Mutuality is the macro-level structural property of a network in which communication exchanges are bidirectional and balanced over time. Two metrics are used: the Harmonic mean reciprocity captures the volume balance between actor pairs, and Reciprocity latency captures the time between a message and its first response. Rising latency is a leading indicator of declining mutuality, consistent with the temporal discounting mechanism in Relational Event Theory.
